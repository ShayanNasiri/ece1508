\begin{abstract}
We investigate whether small language models (SLMs) with fewer than 2 billion parameters can perform multi-choice recipe recommendation, a task requiring commonsense reasoning over natural-language preference queries. Using Recipe-MPR QA, a dataset of 500 multiple-choice questions spanning five query types, we evaluate SmolLM2 models at 135M, 360M, and 1.7B parameters alongside Qwen2.5-3B and DeepSeek-R1-7B. We apply Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) finetuning with systematic ablation over multiple configurations and augment training data with LLM-generated synthetic examples. Our results reveal a sharp capability threshold between 360M and 1.7B parameters: below it, all models perform at chance level ($\sim$20\%) regardless of finetuning or augmentation. Above it, finetuning yields a 60-percentage-point improvement (18.7\% to 78.7\% at 1.7B), and synthetic data augmentation provides an additional 4-percentage-point gain to 82.7\%. Critically, the same synthetic augmentation produces no improvement at 360M, confirming that data quality cannot compensate for insufficient model capacity. Accuracy scales further with model size---86.7\% at 3B and 97.3\% at 7B without finetuning. We additionally find that larger models risk catastrophic overfitting on small datasets. These findings demonstrate that a capability threshold governs when finetuning and data augmentation become effective.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Recipe recommendation systems must reason over diverse user preferences expressed in natural language, including specific ingredient requests, commonsense dietary knowledge, negated constraints, analogical comparisons, and temporal meal-planning considerations. While large language models (LLMs) with billions of parameters have demonstrated strong performance on such reasoning tasks~[1,2], deploying them in resource-constrained settings---such as mobile devices or embedded kitchen systems---motivates the investigation of small language models (SLMs) as alternatives.

Recent work on model scaling has established that language model capabilities often emerge discontinuously as parameter counts increase~[3], raising a practical question: what is the minimum model size required for a given reasoning task? This question has important implications for deployment, as smaller models offer advantages in latency, memory footprint, energy consumption, and privacy through on-device inference.

In this work, we address this question in the context of recipe recommendation by evaluating models ranging from 135 million to 7 billion parameters on Recipe-MPR QA, a multiple-choice benchmark requiring preference-based recipe selection. We systematically investigate whether LoRA finetuning~[4], data augmentation, and synthetic data generation can compensate for limited model capacity, and we characterize the scaling behavior across this parameter range.

\section{Preliminaries and Problem Formulation}

\textbf{Task definition.} Given a natural-language query $q$ expressing a recipe preference and a set of five candidate recipes $\{r_1, r_2, r_3, r_4, r_5\}$, the task is to select the recipe $r^*$ that best satisfies the expressed preference. Each query is annotated with one or more of five types: \textit{Specific} (explicit ingredient or technique requests), \textit{Commonsense} (requiring world knowledge about food), \textit{Negated} (constraints expressed through negation), \textit{Analogical} (comparisons to known dishes), and \textit{Temporal} (meal-timing or preparation-time constraints).

\textbf{Dataset.} Recipe-MPR QA consists of 500 multiple-choice questions with deterministic stratified splitting into 350 training, 75 validation, and 75 test examples (seed 1508, stratified by query type signature). Each question includes five recipe options with one correct answer and an expert-written correctness explanation.

\textbf{Low-Rank Adaptation.} LoRA~[4] enables parameter-efficient finetuning by decomposing weight updates as $\Delta W = BA$ where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$, with rank $r \ll \min(d, k)$. This reduces trainable parameters from $d \times k$ to $r(d + k)$, enabling finetuning of large models on limited hardware.

\section{Design}

Our system comprises three components: a data pipeline, a finetuning module, and an evaluation framework.

The \textbf{data pipeline} normalizes the raw 500-question dataset into a structured format with stable identifiers, generates deterministic train/validation/test splits, and supports augmentation through both rule-based query rewrites and LLM-generated synthetic examples. The synthetic pipeline uses OpenAI's API to generate novel query-recipe pairs that are reviewed and filtered for quality, producing 88 diverse examples that augment the 350-example training set.

The \textbf{finetuning module} applies LoRA adapters to pretrained transformer models using the PEFT library~[5]. Training is formulated as causal language modeling on chat-formatted examples, where each training instance consists of a system prompt, the multiple-choice question, and the correct answer letter. We conducted a systematic ablation over six configurations for SmolLM2-135M (Runs A--F), varying LoRA rank ($r \in \{16, 32, 64\}$), learning rate, number of epochs, dropout, and augmentation strategy, followed by Run~G extending the best configuration to SmolLM2-360M.

The \textbf{evaluation framework} is model-agnostic, supporting two backends: Ollama for serving larger models via REST API, and HuggingFace Transformers for direct inference with finetuned adapters. It implements two evaluation modes: generative (parsing free-text responses) and log-likelihood (scoring answer tokens by logits for base models). A fuzzy response parser recovers answers from varied output formats, including DeepSeek's \texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{\}} notation and verbose SmolLM2 responses.

\section{Methodology}

\textbf{Models.} We evaluate five model families spanning two orders of magnitude in parameter count: SmolLM2-135M, SmolLM2-360M, SmolLM2-1.7B~[6], Qwen2.5-3B~[7], and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B~[8]. SmolLM2 models are evaluated in both instruct and base variants where available. All finetuned models use LoRA adapters applied to attention projection matrices.

\textbf{Finetuning ablation.} For SmolLM2-135M, we conducted six training runs (A--F) systematically varying: LoRA rank ($r \in \{16, 32, 64\}$), learning rate ($\{2 \times 10^{-4}, 1 \times 10^{-4}\}$), dropout ($\{0.05, 0.1\}$), number of epochs ($\{5, 10\}$), and inclusion of 160 rule-based augmented examples. All runs used $\alpha = 2r$. Run~C (LoRA $r$=32, $\alpha$=64, learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$, 10 epochs, dropout 0.05) produced the best validation performance and was adopted as the standard configuration for subsequent experiments. We applied this configuration to SmolLM2-360M (Run~G), SmolLM2-1.7B (Run~I), and Qwen2.5-3B. For synthetic augmentation experiments, we used the same LoRA $r$=32, $\alpha$=64, learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$, warmup ratio 0.1, and 10 epochs, applied to SmolLM2-360M (Run~H) and SmolLM2-1.7B (Run~K).

\textbf{Synthetic data generation.} We generated 88 synthetic training examples using an LLM-powered pipeline that produces entirely novel query-recipe combinations, unlike the rule-based augmentations which simply rewrite existing queries. Following guidelines from the model collapse literature on limiting synthetic data ratios, we set the synthetic proportion to 25\%, yielding 88 synthetic examples mixed with the 350 original training examples (438 total). This dataset was used for both Run~H (360M) and Run~K (1.7B), enabling a controlled comparison across the capability threshold.

\textbf{Evaluation protocol.} All models are evaluated on the same 75-question test split. Instruct and finetuned models are evaluated generatively: the model receives a chat-formatted prompt and generates a free-text response parsed for a letter choice. Base (non-instruct) models are evaluated via log-likelihood: for each answer option, we compute the log-probability of the answer token conditioned on the prompt and select the highest-scoring option.

\subsection{Finetuning Ablation at 135M}

The ablation study for SmolLM2-135M (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) reveals that neither LoRA capacity nor augmented data meaningfully improves performance below the capability threshold. Run~C, using LoRA rank 32 without augmentation, achieved the highest accuracy at 22.7\%, while runs incorporating 160 rule-based augmented examples (D, E, F) showed higher validation loss, indicating that the query rewrites introduced overfitting rather than useful signal. Run~G extended the best configuration to SmolLM2-360M, achieving 17.3\%---still at chance level, confirming the threshold.

\subsection{Synthetic Data Augmentation Across the Threshold}

To isolate the effect of synthetic data, we conducted a controlled comparison at two model sizes straddling the capability threshold. At 360M parameters, adding 88 synthetic examples (Run~H, 21.3\%) improved over training on original data alone (Run~G, 17.3\%) by 4 percentage points, but both results remain near random chance (20\%), suggesting the improvement is not meaningful. At 1.7B parameters, the same augmentation (Run~K) improved accuracy from 78.7\% to 82.7\%, also a 4-percentage-point gain but now lifting performance well above chance. Gains at 1.7B were concentrated in Specific queries (86.9\% to 95.7\%) and Analogical queries (75.0\% to 100\%), while Commonsense (74.4\% to 76.9\%) and Negated (82.4\%, unchanged) showed smaller or no gains. This demonstrates that synthetic data augmentation interacts with model capacity: while it produces a nominal improvement at 360M, the effect is only practically meaningful above the capability threshold where the model can exploit the additional training signal.

\subsection{Catastrophic Overfitting at Scale}

Finetuning Qwen2.5-3B with the same configuration used for SmolLM2-1.7B (LoRA $r$=32, $\alpha$=64, learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$, 10 epochs) produced catastrophic overfitting: training loss dropped to near zero within the first epoch, indicating complete memorization of the 350-example training set. The model achieved 0\% accuracy with 75 parse failures. A conservative retraining (learning rate $1 \times 10^{-5}$, LoRA $r$=8, $\alpha$=16, 2 epochs) partially recovered to 53.3\% but still represented a 33-percentage-point degradation from the 86.7\% zero-shot baseline. This demonstrates that finetuning on small datasets can be actively harmful for models already performing well on a task.

\section{Discussion}

Our results reveal three key insights about the relationship between model scale and task-specific capability for recipe recommendation.

First, the capability threshold between 360M and 1.7B parameters is notably sharp rather than gradual. This is consistent with the emergent abilities hypothesis~[3], which predicts that certain capabilities appear discontinuously with scale. The fact that SmolLM2-1.7B-Instruct scores 18.7\% without finetuning---identical to the 135M and 360M models---suggests that the relevant knowledge is not accessible through prompting alone but can be unlocked through task-specific training once sufficient capacity exists.

Second, our synthetic data experiments reveal a nuanced interaction between augmentation and model capacity. Below the threshold, neither rule-based augmentation (160 query rewrites) nor LLM-generated synthetic data (88 examples) pushed models beyond chance performance, confirming that the bottleneck is representational capacity. Above the threshold, the same synthetic data yielded a 4-percentage-point improvement at 1.7B (78.7\% to 82.7\%), demonstrating that augmentation is effective precisely when the model can exploit the additional signal. This asymmetry implies practitioners should verify their model exceeds the task's capability threshold before investing in augmentation.

Third, the catastrophic overfitting of Qwen2.5-3B highlights a complementary risk: models with excess capacity relative to the training set memorize rather than generalize. This creates an asymmetry where underpowered models cannot learn and overpowered models overfit destructively, suggesting finetuning is most beneficial in a moderate capacity regime. The strong zero-shot performance of Qwen2.5-3B (86.7\%) and DeepSeek-R1-7B (97.3\%) further suggests that for recipe recommendation, models above approximately 3B parameters have already acquired sufficient knowledge during pretraining, making finetuning on small datasets unnecessary or counterproductive.

\section{Conclusions}

We conducted a systematic scaling study of language models from 135M to 7B parameters on recipe preference recommendation, revealing a sharp capability threshold between 360M and 1.7B parameters. Below this threshold, no combination of finetuning, LoRA configuration, or data augmentation produces performance above random chance. Above it, finetuning yields a 60-percentage-point gain at 1.7B, and synthetic data augmentation provides an additional 4-percentage-point improvement---but only above the threshold. Larger pretrained models perform well without task-specific training (86.7\% at 3B, 97.3\% at 7B zero-shot). Our results provide practical guidance: practitioners should verify that their target model size exceeds the task's capability threshold before investing in finetuning or augmentation pipelines. Future work could investigate the threshold more precisely (e.g., at 500M and 1B parameters), explore knowledge distillation from larger to smaller models, and extend the analysis to other commonsense reasoning benchmarks.
